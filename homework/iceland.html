Velkomin til Íslands

The Majesty of Nature

Iceland, Known as the "Land of Fire and Ice," Iceland is a volcanic island shaped by massive glaciers and active volcanoes. Visitors marvel at the Blue Lagoon's geothermal waters, erupting geysers, and Skógafoss waterfall. From the dancing Northern Lights to vast black sand beaches, its otherworldly landscapes offer an unparalleled escape into raw, untouched wilderness.

Heritage and Modernity

Settled by Vikings over 1,000 years ago, Iceland boasts the world’s oldest parliament, the Althingi. This resilient nation blends rich folklore with modern innovation, leading the world in renewable energy. Consistently ranked as the world’s most peaceful country, it offers a safe, welcoming culture centered in the vibrant capital, Reykjavík.

Basic Information

Language, Seasons, and Essentials

Iceland’s official language is Icelandic, though English is spoken fluently by almost everyone. For a safe self-drive adventure, the most essential websites are Safetravel.is for real-time safety alerts and Road.is for live road conditions. Each season offers unique magic: summer (June–August) features the midnight sun and accessible hiking trails, while winter(November–March) is the prime time for witnessing the ethereal Northern Lights amidst dramatic snowy landscapes. Shoulder seasons like spring and autumn provide a balance of fewer crowds and lower costs. 

Currency and Consumption

The official currency is the Icelandic Króna (ISK). While cash is rarely needed because credit and debit cards are accepted everywhere—even for small transactions—it is wise to have a card with a PIN for automated petrol stations. Iceland is known for its high cost of living; expect to pay around 3,300 ISK for an inexpensive restaurant meal. To save money, many travellers shop at local supermarkets like Bónus and rent a car, which remains the most efficient way to explore the island's vast natural wonders. 

Reykjavík

Sólfarið (The Sun Voyager)

Sólfarið (The Sun Voyager) is an iconic stainless-steel sculpture located along Reykjavík's scenic waterfront. Designed by artist Jón Gunnar Árnason, it was unveiled in 1990 to commemorate the city's 200th anniversary. Although its sleek, skeletal frame often resembles a Viking longship, the artist intended it as a "dreamboat" or an ode to the sun. It symbolises the promise of undiscovered territory, hope, and freedom. With Mount Esja as a majestic backdrop, it is one of the city's most photographed spots, especially stunning during the golden hours of sunrise or sunset.

Kolaportið (The Flea Market)

Kolaportið is Iceland’s largest and only year-round flea market, operating every weekend near the Old Harbour. Since 1989, this bustling indoor bazaar has been a treasure trove for locals and tourists seeking authentic Icelandic culture. You can find everything from hand-knit Lopapeysa sweaters and vintage records to rare antiques. The food section is a highlight, offering daring delicacies like fermented shark (hákarl) and dried fish. It’s the perfect place to grab affordable souvenirs, mingle with "characters," and experience the vibrant, unpolished side of life in Reykjavík.

South Iceland

Blue Lagoon (Bláa Lónið)

The Blue Lagoon is a world-renowned geothermal spa located in a dramatic lava field on the Reykjanes Peninsula. Famous for its milky-blue, mineral-rich waters, the lagoon maintains a relaxing temperature of 37–40°C year-round. The water is a unique byproduct of the nearby Svartsengi Geothermal Power Plant, containing high levels of silica and algae known for their skin-healing properties. Visitors can enjoy complimentary silica mud masks and a swim-up bar while surrounded by otherworldly volcanic scenery. It is conveniently located just 20 minutes from Keflavík International Airport, making it a popular first or last stop in Iceland.

Seljalandsfoss Waterfall

Seljalandsfoss is one of Iceland’s most iconic and photographed waterfalls, located right off the Ring Road on the South Coast. Dropping approximately 60 metres from ancient sea cliffs, it is uniquely famous for the walking path that allows visitors to go entirely behind the curtain of water. This 360-degree perspective offers breathtaking views, especially during sunset when the light catches the mist. The water originates from the Eyjafjallajökull glacier volcano, which famously erupted in 2010. Be sure to wear waterproof gear, as the spray is relentless, and the path can be slippery.

East Iceland

Egilsstaðir

Egilsstaðir is the largest town in East Iceland and serves as the region's essential service and transportation hub. Established in 1947 on the banks of the Lagarfljót River, it is a young town steeped in modern folklore. Visitors often flock to the nearby lake to catch a glimpse of the Lagarfljót Wyrm, a legendary serpent said to inhabit the depths. The town is an ideal base for exploring the Hallormsstaðaskógur forest—Iceland's largest—and the stunning Hengifoss waterfall. It’s also the only region in Iceland where you can spot wild reindeer roaming freely in the surrounding highlands.

Hvalnes Nature Reserve & Beach

Hvalnes Nature Reserve is a dramatic coastal gem dominated by the jagged, multi-peaked Eystrahorn mountain. The reserve features a sprawling black pebble beach that stretches for several kilometres, offering a raw and uncrowded alternative to the busier south coast. A highlight of the area is the bright orange Hvalnes Lighthouse, which provides a striking contrast against the dark volcanic landscape. It is a paradise for birdwatchers, especially in summer when whooper swans gather in the lagoon and diverse seabirds nest along the steep, mineral-rich cliffs.

North Iceland

Jólahúsið (The Christmas House)

Jólahúsið, or the Christmas House, is a whimsical, fire-engine red cottage located just a 10-minute drive from Akureyri. This year-round holiday wonderland looks like a giant gingerbread house and is packed with Icelandic Christmas traditions. Inside, visitors can explore two floors of international and local decorations, traditional sweets, and folklore about the thirteen Yule Lads. The surrounding "Christmas Garden" features a fairy-tale tower housing the world’s largest advent calendar and a peaceful wishing well. Whether it’s July or December, the crackling fireplace and festive scents offer a magical escape into the heart of the Yuletide spirit.

Dimmuborgir (The Dark Fortress)

Dimmuborgir is a vast, eerie landscape of extraordinary lava formations located east of Lake Mývatn. Formed 2,300 years ago by a collapsed lava lake, its jagged pillars and caves resemble a medieval stone citadel, earning it the name "Dark Castles." The area is steeped in mythology; some legends claim it as the gateway to hell, while others say it is the home of the Yule Lads and their troll mother, Grýla. Fans of pop culture may recognise its dramatic terrain from Game of Thrones. Several well-marked trails, like the popular Church Path, allow hikers to explore this hauntingly beautiful volcanic labyrinth.
